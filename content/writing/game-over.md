## Fragile systems, failed plans and the people who adapt anyway

_“They’re going to fire me.”_

There was rising panic in her voice.

By that point we’d worked together for years.

We weren’t really just colleagues anymore.

We were friends.

Which made the whole thing feel strangely surreal because barely twenty-four hours earlier I’d been confidently reassuring her that her job was safe.

The exact words from our CTO still rang clearly in my head:

_“No one is getting fired over AI.”_

He’d said it less than a month earlier.

So what the hell was happening?

Now her entire department was disappearing.

Apparently they’d been _“reviewing the role”_ the entire time.

One badly timed hack day later, where she couldn’t find the right project to attach herself to, and suddenly she was being handed a P45.

I thought I’d gotten better at reading the political side of this industry.

Better at spotting danger.

Better at understanding how companies really work.

But moments like that remind you how fragile certainty actually is.

I didn’t see any of it coming.

### Every system breaks eventually

You tell yourself:

_“I understand this person.”_

_“This would never happen.”_

_“We’re solid.”_

Then life blindsides you.

You go in to the kitchen to make a cup of tea and come back to the living room to discover your entire life has burnt to the ground.

The worst part is you didn’t even want the tea that much.

Now you’re just standing in the ruins of something you thought was permanent.

Systems fail.

Relationships end unexpectedly.

The cathedral you built might never have a single visitor.

And in 2026, it feels like the world is changing faster than your confidence can keep up with.

### “Game over, man. Game over!”

There’s a scene in _Aliens_ that’s stayed with me for years.

The rescue ship is descending.

The survivors are exhausted but hopeful.

Finally, a way out.

Then suddenly the ship lurches sideways out of the sky and explodes in a fireball.

Their escape route is gone in seconds.

That’s when Hudson completely loses it.

_“Game over, man. Game over!”_

The marine suddenly finds himself trapped in a scenario he never trained for.

The future he’d already pictured in his head disappeared instantly.

It’s pathetic yet funny. Endearingly human.

But I always remember what happens next.

Hicks grabs Hudson and almost rips him out of his body armour.

_“Are you finished?”_

Then the camera cuts to Ripley.

Terrified.

But calm.

Because unlike everyone else, she understands something important: _the plan was never the thing keeping them alive._

The people who survive aren’t always the smartest or the most confident.

Sometimes they’re just the ones willing to accept reality quickly enough to adapt to it.
